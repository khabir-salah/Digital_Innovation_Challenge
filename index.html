Reviving Calabar South

Unlocking the Lost Potential of Calabar Port Market

A story of infrastructure needs and immense opportunity in Cross River State

Historical Significance: From Prosperity to Neglect

Calabar South, during the reign of Nigeria's first government, thrived as a hub for international trade. At the heart of this economic activity was the Calabar Port, which served as a key point for import and export. The waterfront was not only vital for generating revenue but also attracted foreign settlers, whose abandoned homes still stand today. This bustling trade center was a source of pride for the state.

However, the once-vibrant Calabar Port Market is now a shadow of its former self. Neglect has turned it into a marketplace for petty traders, overshadowing its potential as a significant economic asset. The river that runs through Calabar South, which could serve as both a transportation route and a tourist attraction, lies underutilized.

Infrastructure Needs

The most pressing need is the rehabilitation of the Calabar Port Market and its surrounding infrastructure. The area has long lacked proper maintenance, and without urgent intervention, it risks further degradation.

  • Roads leading to the market are in disrepair.
  • Basic facilities like water supply, sanitation, and waste management are insufficient or non-existent.
  • The old foreign residences, relics of the colonial era, remain abandoned and could be restored for modern use.

Opportunities

Despite the challenges, Calabar South is ripe with opportunity:

  • Employment: By redeveloping the market, hundreds of jobs could be created, ranging from construction to market management.
  • Transportation: The river could serve as a transportation route, alleviating pressure on roads.
  • Revenue Generation: Like Tin Can Island Port in Lagos, a revitalized Calabar Port could become a revenue hub.
  • Cultural Tourism: Historical buildings could attract tourists, boosting the local economy.
